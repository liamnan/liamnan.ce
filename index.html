Aphrodite 1 - Theoretical Space Station

For this project, I was the lead of the Thermal Analysis team. I worked on design, integration, and validation for the AC and radiator systems. I created an 8 DOF robotic arm, coined the "Aphrodite Autonomous Arm", or AAA, which could "walk" around the station. I researched and selected all interior and exterior materials, focusing on thermal optimization and impact resistance. I created all the grahics and informatics, and significantly contributed to the creation of the final report.

Project Aphrodite is a series of missions my ASU Capstone team explored to prepare humans for extended space travel. We began with a mission to send a crew on a flyby around Venus, but abandoned the original mission due to the limitations of modern technology. After pivoting, we decided to focus on solving the primary health issues associated with long term space habitation by creating a LEO space station with a large centrifuge. This was Aphrodite 1. The following Aphrodite missions would focus on integrating this technology into manned spacecraft, expanding the limits of human exploration.

Ramjet and Scramjet Flow - MATLAB

Developed a MATLAB tool that calculates thrust, efficiency, temperatures, pressures, and entropy changes to identify thermodynamic states and performance trends of non-isentropic ram-compression Brayton Cycle engines.

Descent and Payload Delivery Vehicle

I worked with a team of engineers to develop a descent vehicle that safely deployed a payload after being dropped 100 feet by a drone. For this project, I was the lead engineer; I created all the CAD models, designed the descent and deployment subsystems, and created all the graphics. My team tested several lander body and parachute designs before the final version. Iterative design and testing were a strong emphasis to make sure the lander succeeded.

This is the Preliminary Design Review (PDR) for the project.

RS-25 (LRE) Analysis

Used non-isentropic flow equations to complete a full analysis of oxidizer (LOX) and fuel (LH2) flow through the RS-25 Liquid Rocket Engine (LRE), which was used in the NASA Space Shuttle Program and SLS. I used an Excel sheet with hundreds of cells to calculate the thermodynamic parameters of the flow at over a dozen locations in the engine, including within the pump systems, preburners, and the nozzle. I was able to use this tool to identify LRE performance trends and limitations.
